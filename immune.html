Introduction

The immune system is a complex network of cells, tissues, and organs that work together to protect the body from infection. It is made up of two main components: the innate immune system and the adaptive immune system.

Innate Immune System

The innate immune system is the body's first line of defense against infection. It is made up of a variety of cells and mechanisms that work to identify and destroy foreign invaders, such as bacteria, viruses, and fungi.

  • Phagocytes: These cells engulf and destroy foreign particles.
  • Natural killer cells: These cells kill cells that are infected with viruses or are otherwise damaged.
  • Cytokines: These proteins help to coordinate the immune response.
  • Complement proteins: These proteins help to destroy bacteria and viruses.

Adaptive Immune System

The adaptive immune system is the body's second line of defense against infection. It is made up of a variety of cells and mechanisms that work together to identify and destroy specific pathogens.

  • B cells: These cells produce antibodies, which are proteins that bind to specific antigens on pathogens.
  • T cells: These cells kill cells that are infected with viruses or are otherwise damaged.
  • Memory cells: These cells remember past infections and help the body to mount a faster and more effective response to future infections.

Disorders of the Immune System

The immune system can be weakened by a variety of factors, including stress, malnutrition, and certain medications. This can lead to a variety of disorders, including:

  • Autoimmune diseases: These diseases occur when the immune system attacks the body's own tissues.
  • Immunodeficiencies: These disorders occur when the immune system is unable to fight off infection.
  • Allergies: These reactions occur when the immune system overreacts to a harmless substance.
